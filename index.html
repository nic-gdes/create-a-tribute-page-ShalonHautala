About the Band

Metallica, an iconic and influential rock band hailing from Los Angeles, California, has left an indelible mark on the music landscape since its formation in 1981. With their thunderous blend of heavy metal and thrash, characterized by lightning-fast guitar riffs, aggressive drumming, and emotionally charged lyrics, Metallica has become synonymous with the genre's evolution. From their groundbreaking debut album "Kill 'Em All" to chart-topping hits like "Enter Sandman" and "Nothing Else Matters," the band's powerful sound and dynamic stage presence have garnered them a devoted global fanbase and a lasting legacy as one of the greatest rock acts of all time.

Discography

The following is a list of studio albums only, listed in order of release.

Kill 'Em All

(1983)

Metallica's initial album was originally intended to be titled "Metal Up Your Ass," a phrase commonly seen on their concert posters along with artwork featuring a knife emerging from a toilet. However, Megaforce Records grew hesitant due to the graphic nature of the imagery and title. In response, Cliff Burton expressed anger, leading to the album being named "Kill 'Em All." The cover art was subsequently changed to depict a blood-covered sledgehammer, designed by Gary L. Heard.

Ride the Lightning

(1984)

"Ride The Lightning," Metallica's second album, was named by guitarist Kirk Hammett after reading Stephen King's The Stand, where the term symbolized execution by electric chair. This idea resonated with both Hammett and James Hetfield, inspiring the album's title track. The album's blue cover, featuring an electric chair, was designed by AD Artists. In France, a printing mistake turned the cover green in the initial pressings, resulting in these versions becoming valuable collector's items today.

Master of Puppets

(1986)

In the mid-1985, Metallica, experiencing rising success, enlisted artist Don Brautigam to create the cover art for their upcoming third album. Don, known for his work with notable figures like Chuck Berry and Stephen King, completed the acrylic painting in just three days based on a sketch by James Hetfield. The artwork depicts a scene reminiscent of Arlington National Cemetery, with hands in the sky symbolizing the central theme of manipulation explored in "Master of Puppets," addressing themes like military influence in "Disposable Heroes," oppressive medical figures in "Welcome Home (Sanitarium)," and substance abuse in the title track.

...And Justice for All

(1988)

Metallica's album "...And Justice For All," renowned for its social commentaries, presents a powerful political statement. The album cover, designed by Stephen Gorman, features a shattered and restrained Lady Justice depicted topless, with money falling from her scales. Gorman, who had previously created the discarded "Metal Up Your Ass" cover, drew inspiration from a Munich statue. This photorealistic artwork, complementing the album's bold manifesto, aligns with the themes of the title track and "One," and Stephen's brother Roger had recently photographed the band, including new bassist Jason Newsted, for the EP cover of "$5.98 Covers."

Metallica (Black Album)

(1991)

Metallica's album "...And Justice For All" showcased intricate compositions with lengthy tracks and varied themes. The band's subsequent album, often referred to as the Black Album, shifted towards simplicity by focusing on hooks and deliberate, slower rhythms, a departure from their earlier extravagance. The album cover followed suit, with a predominantly black design featuring a dark grey band logo in the top left corner and a coiled rattlesnake in the bottom right. The snake, taken from the Gadsden flag, tied into the album's lyrics inspired by the American War of Independence, particularly in the song "Don't Tread On Me." Despite these changes, even with the new approach, Spinal Tap remained unimpressed.

Load

(1996)

Metallica's album "Load," released in 1996, marked a significant shift in the band's musical direction, adopting a more alternative rock sound with bluesy influences. Departing from their thrash metal roots, the album showcased a diverse range of tracks characterized by groove-oriented rhythms and introspective lyrics. The album's cover art, a departure from their usual aesthetic, featured a close-up photograph of a coiled blood-red substance against a white background, sparking discussions about the band's evolving image and sound. The ever-inflammatory Andres Serrano designed the album, a photograph of bovine blood mixed with the artist’s own semen. The departure from their traditional style and the provocative cover ignited both curiosity and debates among fans and critics alike. Apparently all of this was Kirk Hammett and Lars Ulrich’s idea. James Hetfield, on the other hand, has called Load’s artwork a “piss-take” in interviews.

ReLoad

(1997)

Released in 1997, Metallica's album "ReLoad" acted as a follow-up to their previous record "Load," continuing the band's exploration of alternative rock elements alongside hard rock and blues influences. With tracks that featured intricate guitar work and introspective themes, the album showcased a diverse sonic palette. The cover art maintained a similar style to "Load", again designed by artist Andres Serrano, featuring an image of yellow and red substances, swirling together against a black background. It was later revealed to be a close-up photo of bovine blood and the artist's own urine. The album's sound and visual direction furthered Metallica's departure from their earlier thrash metal roots, inviting both curiosity and discussions among fans and observers about the band's evolving identity.

St. Anger

(2003)

St. Anger, often considered a low point in Metallica's career, marked a return to simplicity following the departure of long-time bassist Jason Newsted. While criticized for its raw sound and poor production, the album's artwork, esigned by Brian Schroeder, featured a striking image of a clenched fist in vivid reds and oranges. Originally planned as a set of four color variants in the style of Andy Warhol, this concept was ultimately abandoned.

Death Magnetic

(2008)

Following the mixed reception of 'St. Anger,' Metallica returned to their thrash roots with 'Death Magnetic.' The album recaptured the elements that fans initially loved about the band, featuring powerful anthems, progressive ballads, and lightning-fast guitar work, all tied together by its bold title. James Hetfield explained that 'Death Magnetic' began as a tribute to those in the music industry who had passed away, like Layne Staley, symbolizing death as a magnetic force drawing people towards it. The album's visual concept, designed by Turner Duckworth, depicted a coffin surrounded by magnetic-wave-like formations of dirt.

Hardwired to Self-Destruct

(2016)

The artwork for Metallica's 'Hardwired... to Self-Destruct' album features a surreal composite of the four band members' faces. Studio Herring & Herring designed it, drawing inspiration from a similar creation they had made as a wedding gift for Lars Ulrich in 2015. The artwork's abstract nature even left the band members themselves puzzled, as they tried to identify their own facial features within the composition. It became a topic of discussion among the band members, with each recognizing some elements but still finding the overall result enigmatic.

72 Seasons

(2023)

James Hetfield describes '72 Seasons' as an exploration of the formative first 18 years of one's life, shaping their true or false selves. Musically, the album is poised to pay homage to the punk and new wave of British heavy metal influences that inspired the band during their early years leading into 'Kill 'Em All.' The album cover fittingly depicts a cot surrounded by childhood and heavy metal memorabilia, including guitars, headphones, skateboards, toys, and even bullets, against a vibrant yellow backdrop. This artistic scene was created by the design team Turner Duckworth, known for their work on 'Death Magnetic' and 'Lulu', one of the band's live albums.
